Sebring: A Racing Legacy

From WWII airbase to endurance racing icon - the remarkable history of America's oldest road racing circuit.

The concrete runways of Hendricks Army Airfield have witnessed more racing history than almost any venue in America. Since 1950, Sebring International Raceway has challenged drivers with its unforgiving surface and relentless Florida heat.

Military Origins

During World War II, the Sebring airfield trained B-17 crews for combat in Europe. The long runways and taxiways, built to withstand heavy bombers, would prove ideal for a different kind of competition once the war ended.

When Alec Ulmann organized the first 12 Hours of Sebring in 1952, he created what would become America's premier endurance race. European manufacturers immediately recognized the event's potential‚ÄîFerrari, Aston Martin, and Jaguar all competed in those early years.

The Golden Era

The 1950s and 1960s represented Sebring's golden age. The race attracted the world's greatest drivers‚ÄîStirling Moss, Phil Hill, Dan Gurney, and countless others tested themselves against the challenging circuit.

"Sebring is the roughest track I've ever raced on. It destroys cars and punishes drivers. But there's no other place I'd rather race."

‚Äî Phil Hill, 1961 F1 World Champion

The bumpy concrete, unchanged from its military construction, made Sebring unique among racing venues. Cars that couldn't survive 12 hours over the rough surface simply didn't win.

Circuit Evolution

Over the decades, Sebring's layout has evolved while maintaining its essential character. The famous hairpin, the long back straight, and the challenging infield sections remain recognizable to fans of any era.

Key circuit characteristics:

  • Length: 3.74 miles (current configuration)
  • Surface: Mix of asphalt and original concrete
  • Turns: 17 corners with varied camber
  • Challenge: Extreme bumpiness tests suspension durability

The Modern Era

Today, Sebring hosts both IMSA's WeatherTech Championship and FIA WEC rounds. The Super Sebring weekend combines the 12 Hours with WEC's 1000 Miles of Sebring, creating an endurance racing festival.

Modern prototypes attack the circuit at speeds that would astonish the pioneers of the 1950s. Yet the fundamental challenge remains unchanged‚Äîsurvive the bumps, manage the heat, and pace yourself for 12 grueling hours.

Atmosphere

Sebring's atmosphere is unlike any other racing event. The infield camping creates a festival environment, with fans enjoying the unique access that characterizes American motorsport. The transition from day to night, as cars circulate under the Florida stars, creates memories that last lifetimes.

Looking Forward

As Sebring approaches its 75th anniversary, discussions continue about circuit improvements while preserving the venue's essential character. The bumps, so often criticized by drivers, have become part of the legend‚Äîremoving them would fundamentally change what makes Sebring special.

America's oldest permanent road racing circuit continues to write new chapters in motorsport history. From WWII airbase to endurance racing shrine, Sebring's journey reflects the evolution of American motorsport itself.
