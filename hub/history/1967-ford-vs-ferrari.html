1967: When Ford Conquered Ferrari

The legendary Le Mans battle that changed motorsport forever - and inspired a Hollywood blockbuster.

11 min read
Ford GT40 Ferrari Le Mans History 1960s Racing

The war began with a failed acquisition. In 1963, Henry Ford II believed he had a deal to buy Ferrari. When Enzo Ferrari walked away at the last moment, Ford channeled his fury into a single goal: beat Ferrari at Le Mans.

The Birth of the GT40

Ford assembled an all-star team to create a Ferrari killer. Carroll Shelby, fresh from his Cobra success, led the American effort. British engineering expertise came from Lola and ex-Aston Martin personnel. The result was the GT40‚Äîa name derived from its 40-inch height.

Early development was brutal. The car was fast but fragile. Le Mans 1964 saw all Ford entries retire. 1965 brought more failures. Critics wondered if American ingenuity could ever match European racing heritage.

The 1966 Breakthrough

Everything changed in 1966. The Mark II GT40, powered by a 7.0-liter V8, was finally reliable enough to race the distance. Ford entered the race with a massive effort‚Äîfactory cars supported by unlimited resources.

"We're going to beat Ferrari if it's the last thing we do."

‚Äî Henry Ford II

The result was historic: Ford finished 1-2-3, with the Bruce McLaren/Chris Amon car crossing the line first. Ferrari's dominance at Le Mans was shattered. But controversy over a forced photo finish marred the celebration, setting the stage for 1967's redemption.

1967: The Complete Victory

If 1966 was the breakthrough, 1967 was the coronation. Ford returned with the all-American crew of Dan Gurney and A.J. Foyt in the lead Mark IV. The car represented the ultimate evolution of the GT program‚Äîlighter, more powerful, and refined through years of racing development.

The race saw typically dramatic Le Mans attrition. Ferraris retired. Other Fords fell away with mechanical troubles. But the Gurney/Foyt car ran with metronomic precision, building an insurmountable lead.

Victory and Tradition

When Gurney took the checkered flag, he established a tradition that endures to this day. Celebrating on the podium, he spontaneously sprayed champagne over the crowd‚Äîthe first recorded champagne celebration in motorsport history.

The statistics from 1967 are remarkable:

  • Distance: 5,232 kilometers in 24 hours
  • Average Speed: 218.03 km/h‚Äîa new record
  • Winning Margin: Four laps ahead of second place
  • All-American Crew: First time in Le Mans history

The Aftermath

Ford's Le Mans campaign cost over $25 million in 1960s dollars‚Äîhundreds of millions by today's standards. But the payoff was incalculable. The GT40 became a cultural icon, symbolizing American engineering ambition.

Ferrari, humbled but not defeated, would return stronger. The Italian team evolved their prototypes and eventually reclaimed Le Mans glory. But they never forgot the years when an American corporation out-spent and out-raced them at their own game.

Legacy

The Ford vs Ferrari rivalry transcends motorsport. It represents the eternal tension between passionate craftsmen and industrial giants. The 2019 film brought the story to new audiences, but those who witnessed the original battles needed no dramatization.

Today, as Ford returns to Le Mans with the new GT, echoes of 1967 resonate through the paddock. The spirit of Dan Gurney, champagne bottle in hand, still inspires everyone who dares to challenge the establishment.
